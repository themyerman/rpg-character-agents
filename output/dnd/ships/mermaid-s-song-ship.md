*Sloop — a pleasure yacht three owners removed from her gilded youth, still pretending she isn't carrying secrets*

### Description
From a cable's length she still cuts a pretty silhouette — long, low, raked masts and a figurehead of a mermaid whose painted scales have been touched up so many times the colors no longer agree. Step aboard and the illusion fractures: scorched planking near the aft companionway has been sanded smooth but not replaced, and the brass fittings along the rail are mismatched, replaced piecemeal by hands that didn't care to match the originals. The interior cabins still smell faintly of cedar and rosewater under the tar, and there is a small ballroom belowdecks that has been converted, badly, into a cargo hold — the chandelier hooks are still in the ceiling. Sailors who have served on her say the ship listens; she creaks differently when strangers are aboard.

### History
She was laid down eight years ago by the Desta family of the inland river-houses, commissioned by **Lady Miriam Desta** as a wedding gift to her daughter — a vessel meant for summer cruises along the coastal estates, dressed in silk awnings and never far from a friendly harbor. The disgrace came within two years. No one outside the family will speak the particulars, but the daughter vanished, the marriage was annulled posthumously, and the *Mermaid's Song* was sold at auction in a single afternoon to a merchant consortium that wanted the hull more than the history. Under her second owner, **Captain Luyu Yellowbird**, she ran legitimate coastal trade for three seasons before a fire in the forward cabins gutted half her interior and killed four of the crew. The cause was never determined. Yellowbird walked away from her at the next port and was last heard of working a riverboat.

Her third sale happened quietly, between parties who do not appear on any harbor registry, and the bill of sale in her current papers lists only initials. **Captain Mika Runningwater** has had the helm for nineteen months now and answers questions about provenance with a flat smile and a change of subject. The ballroom-turned-hold has been reinforced with hasty timber bracing. The original silver service has been sold off piece by piece. What remains of her pleasure-yacht origins — the cedar paneling, the etched-glass lamps in the captain's cabin, the absurd carved cherubs on the stern rail — Runningwater has kept, not out of sentiment but because tearing them out would cost money the ship does not have.

### Quirks
- **The Drunkard's Lean:** She heels to port in dead calm and starboard in heavy weather, the opposite of every other ship her size. Experienced hands shrug; new sailors get seasick at anchor. Runningwater claims it's the ballast and won't let anyone investigate further.
- **The Listening Cabins:** The aft passenger cabins, built for whispered noble gossip, carry sound strangely. A conversation in the captain's quarters can be heard clearly in the third cabin down — and vice versa. The crew knows which cabins not to be assigned.
- **The Chandelier Hooks:** Six iron rings still hang from the cargo hold ceiling where the ballroom chandeliers once swung. The crew uses them to suspend hammocks, lanterns, and — once, memorably — a prisoner.
- **The Burn That Won't Paint Over:** A patch of decking near the aft companionway refuses to take stain, varnish, or paint evenly. Whatever burned there burned hot. In certain light it almost looks like the outline of a person.
- **The Figurehead's Hand:** The mermaid figurehead has been repaired so many times that her left hand is now a different wood than the rest of her — pale ash where the body is dark oak. Sailors touch it for luck before going aloft. No one remembers starting the habit.

### Crew Roles
- **Captain** — Currently Mika Runningwater. The role on this ship demands a particular tolerance for not asking questions, and a particular skill at deflecting them when asked.
- **Sailing Master** — Must know the Drunkard's Lean intimately. A new sailing master has wrecked her twice in shallow water before learning to read her backwards-heel.
- **Hold Master** — A position invented by Runningwater. Manages what goes into the converted ballroom-hold, what comes out, and which manifests the harbor master sees. Trusted absolutely or not at all.
- **Carpenter** — Spends most of her time on the burn-scarred sections, which keep developing new soft spots. She has stopped trying to match the original cedar and is rebuilding in whatever's cheap.
- **Quartermaster** — Responsible for the gun crews on her sixteen mounts, which is more weaponry than a former pleasure yacht has any business carrying. Drills them at night, away from port.
- **Cabin Steward** — A holdover role from her noble days. Currently kept on because the listening cabins need a crewmember who knows which ones to put guests in, and which ones to put guests in when you want to hear what they say.

### Current Situation
She is three days out from making port at a mid-sized harbor town, with a hold full of crates that Runningwater's manifest lists as "preserved foodstuffs, textiles, and assorted dry goods" and that the crew has been told, with some emphasis, is legitimate. The harbor master at their destination has been flagged by someone — a rival, an informant, a creditor — and is preparing to conduct a full inspection rather than the usual cursory one. Runningwater knows. The crew suspects. The cargo, whatever it actually is, was loaded at night from a longboat that flew no colors. For a buyer she's a bargain hull with a story; for a hirer she's a fast sloop with sixteen guns and a captain who won't ask where you're going; for a boarder she's a floating question that nobody has yet answered out loud.

### GM Notes
*The fire that gutted her under Captain Yellowbird was not an accident, and it was not the first time someone tried to destroy her. The Desta daughter did not vanish — she died aboard this ship, in what is now the cargo hold, and the family paid a great deal of money to make the manner of her death disappear along with the body. Someone who was there is still alive and still looking for the ship, not to recover anything but to finish burning her to the waterline. When that person catches up — at the inspection, in the next port, or aboard as a passenger under a false name — the burn that won't paint over will start to smell like smoke again, and the listening cabins will carry a voice no living crewmember put there.*
