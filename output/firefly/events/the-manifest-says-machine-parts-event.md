## **The Manifest Says Machine Parts**
*Fires during a quiet planetside cargo pickup at night — the wrong crate gives itself away before the crew lifts off.*

### When It Fires
The crew has set down at a private agricultural strip on a Border world — somewhere like Jiangyin or Beaumonde's quieter cousin — to pick up six crates of "irrigation pump assemblies" for a milk run to the next moon over. The contact, a soft-spoken broker named **Nira Suriyawong**, paid half up front and left them to the night-shift loaders. It's past midnight. Most of the crew is asleep or playing cards. One crewman is on watch by the cargo ramp, listening to crickets and the tick of cooling metal.

### What Happens
A sound from inside one of the crates — not mechanical settling. A wet cough. Then a child's voice, hushed by an adult, then silence again. The crate is the fifth of six, banded and sealed with an agri-co-op stamp that no longer quite matches the others on close inspection. Inside, if pried open: a man in his thirties, **Wayra Flores**, his pregnant wife, and a girl of maybe seven. War-era Independent tattoo on the man's forearm, badly covered with ink. They are running from something, and they thought they had another twelve hours before anyone opened the box.

### The Party's Position
The crew knows they were paid well — *too* well, in retrospect — for a routine pump delivery, and that Suriyawong was specific about not opening the crates "for insurance reasons." They do **not** yet know who is hunting the Flores family, that a private security cutter is already three hours out on a tip, or that the destination moon is where the buyer expects to *take delivery* of the family, not the pumps.

### Possible Responses
1. **Reseal the crate, lift off, deliver as contracted.** The job pays. The family is somebody else's problem — until the crew sees what's waiting on the receiving pad, and has to decide whether they can stomach watching the handoff. This is the path that costs the most quietly.
2. **Confront Suriyawong before lifting off.** She's still in town, drinking at a roadhouse two klicks away. She'll talk if pressed — but she's small fish, and the people she works for have ears on this strip. Asking questions out loud is a decision in itself.
3. **Hide the family, fake the manifest, fly somewhere that isn't the destination.** Generous, dangerous, and expensive. Wayra has a name of a contact on Persephone, **Canek Mixtli**, who he swears can get them lost — but the crew has to eat the burn-fee on the contract, dodge the cutter, and trust a man who's already lied to them by climbing into a crate.

### Complications
- The seven-year-old is sick — a real cough, fever rising. Whatever they're running from, she needs more than a hiding place; she needs a medic within a day or two. Wayra knew this when he sealed the crate and gambled anyway.
- The "buyer" on the destination moon isn't a slaver or a bounty hunter in the conventional sense. He's a retired Alliance major who lost a son at Serenity Valley, and Wayra Flores was the Independent sergeant who gave the order that killed him. This isn't trafficking. It's a private execution, paid for through legitimate-looking shipping channels, and the major has the paperwork to make it look lawful when it's done.

### GM Notes
Let the cough land before anyone decides anything — the event works because the crate stops being cargo the moment they hear the child. If the party tries to ignore it and complete the run, escalate by having the family make noise on approach; don't let them off easy with passive avoidance. Leave the major unresolved: even if the family escapes, he has the manifest, the ship's registry, and the patience of a grieving man with money.
