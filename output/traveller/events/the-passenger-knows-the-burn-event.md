## **The Passenger Knows the Burn**

*Mid-skim at a gas giant, a paying passenger casually asks the pilot about a course correction that hasn't been mentioned to anyone aboard.*

### When It Fires

The ship is three hours into a fuel skim at an unremarkable gas giant, well off the main shipping lanes — the kind of detour a working crew makes to save credits on refined fuel at the next port. The scoops are deployed, the hull is creaking under atmospheric stress, and someone on the bridge is watching the temperature gauges instead of paying attention to the lounge. A middle passenger named Yaw Asante — booked at Tukera rates, paid in cash, polite, unremarkable — wanders up with a cup of something hot and asks if the captain has decided yet whether to take the longer 142-degree burn out of the well or risk the tighter one that shaves four hours but stresses the manoeuvre drive.

### What Happens

Nobody told him about either option. That conversation happened on the bridge between the pilot and the engineer six hours ago, with the intercom off. Asante asks the question the way you ask about the weather — eyes on his cup, half-smiling — and then looks up when the silence runs a beat too long. He recovers smoothly: "Sorry — I overheard one of you mention it earlier. Just curious." But the pilot knows exactly when she said it, and to whom, and Asante wasn't in the compartment.

### The Party's Position

The crew knows their passenger has information he shouldn't have. They don't know whether he's surveilling the ship, surveilling *them*, working for a rival shipper, an Imperial agency, a creditor, or simply a retired spacer who's spent decades reading drive harmonics and guessed correctly from the sound of the plant. Any of those is plausible. Only one is harmless.

### Possible Responses

1. **Confront him directly.** Pull him aside, ask flat questions, watch his face. He'll have a story ready — he always does — but pressure may crack it, or it may force whatever he's doing here to accelerate. If he's armed or has accomplices among the other passengers, the crew has just announced they've noticed.
2. **Quietly search his cabin while he's at dinner.** Engineer or steward can manage it during the next meal. What turns up — a comm unit tuned to an unusual band, a sealed data wafer, nothing at all — defines the rest of the voyage. Risk: he notices the search and the game changes shape without the crew knowing it has.
3. **Feed him bad information and watch where it goes.** Mention a fake course decision in his hearing and monitor outbound comms traffic for the next jump. Slowest option, most informative, requires the crew to keep their nerve through a week of jump with a man they no longer trust eating at their table.

### Complications

- **There's a bug in the bridge.** Small, adhesive, tucked behind a conduit run where nobody looks during pre-flight. It's been there longer than Asante has — he's the *receiver*, not the planter. Someone in the ship's last port of call put it there, and Asante boarded specifically to collect what it heard. The crew has been carrying it for at least one prior jump.
- **Asante isn't his real name, but his booking documents are real.** The Tukera passenger manifest will check out. The face won't match any criminal database the crew can access. He's working for someone with enough reach to issue clean identity papers — which narrows the field to corporate intelligence, a noble house, or an Imperial service. None of those are people you want to inconvenience, and all of them have longer memories than the crew has runway.

### GM Notes

Fire this during the quiet stretch after the skim, when players are relaxing and expecting transit to be uneventful — the contrast is the whole point. If the party ignores the slip and never investigates, let Asante disembark at the next port without incident, and have the consequences arrive two or three sessions later when a transcript of bridge conversation appears in the hands of someone who shouldn't have it. Leave unresolved: who placed the bug, and at which port. That's a thread you can pull on for a long time.
