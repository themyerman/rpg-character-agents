## **The Preacher's Wife Is Back**
*Fires when the posse is passing through a small settlement, as a woman walks into town from the east road — a woman three people in town will quietly tell you died of fever eighteen months ago.*

### When It Fires
The posse rides into Sagewick, a settlement of maybe forty people — a general store, a smithy, a church, a cluster of houses. Nothing notable, a stop for water and provisions. While they're at the store, they notice a cluster of people near the church — not a crowd, just a few residents standing at a distance, watching a woman hang laundry in the yard of the manse. One of the residents, without being asked, says: "That's Eliza Crane. Wife of the Reverend." A pause. "She died of fever last winter."

### What Happens
Eliza Crane is alive, physically present, and apparently unremarkable: mid-thirties, brown hair, a practical dress, methodical about her laundry. She does not appear ill, does not appear disturbed, and greets the posse if they approach with a smile and the normal pleasantries of a small-town woman meeting travelers. She says she was away — "I needed time with my sister in Abilene." She does not explain the death. She speaks of her husband, Reverend Thomas Crane, with affection. Crane himself is inside the church, and the light is on, and through the window the posse can see him sitting in a pew facing the altar, completely still, in a posture that looks less like prayer and more like a man waiting for something.

### The Party's Position
They don't know Eliza Crane, have no stake in Sagewick, and can ride out in twenty minutes. Nothing has happened. A woman has come home. The residents who are watching are doing so quietly, from a distance, and not interfering — either because they believe the "Abilene" explanation, or because they're afraid, or because in the Weird West you learn not to push on a thing like this before you understand what you're pushing on.

### Possible Responses
1. **Leave.** They got their water. Whatever happened in Sagewick is Sagewick's problem. Though if anyone in the posse has a Faith trait and rolls well, they feel something — not danger exactly, but wrongness — as they ride past the church. The feeling fades at the edge of town. The posse is two hours east when they hear, faintly, the church bell ring. Once.
2. **Ask around.** The general store owner, Bram Holz, will tell them: Eliza Crane died February last, buried in the churchyard, Crane presided over the service himself. He went quiet after. Started conducting services with the doors closed. Three families stopped attending. The grave is in the churchyard. The posse can check the churchyard. The headstone is there. The ground in front of it is undisturbed.
3. **Talk to Crane.** He comes to the door when they knock, after a long pause. He looks at them with the expression of a man who is simultaneously desperate for help and terrified of what that help would require. He says: "She came back. She came back, and I — I couldn't — God forgave the prodigal son. God can —" He doesn't finish. He looks past them, at Eliza in the yard, still hanging laundry. He says, very quietly: "She hums now. She didn't used to hum."

### Complications
- The grave is intact — undisturbed soil, proper depth. If the posse's hexslinger or anyone with arcane senses examines the grave, it's clean. There's nothing supernatural about that patch of dirt. There's a body in it. Which means either Eliza wasn't in it, or the woman in the yard came from somewhere else.
- The humming that Crane mentioned: two residents will confirm it independently. She hums while she works — always the same melody, which one resident can whistle back if asked. A blessed character or anyone with knowledge of frontier hymns recognizes it: it's a burial hymn. Not a common one. A specific one used by a tradition out of the deep South for the interment of plague dead. Eliza Crane grew up in Ohio.
- At sunset, if the posse is still in Sagewick, Eliza knocks on the door of the residence where they're staying (if they took a room) and brings them a plate of food. She is warm, gracious, slightly too calm. She says: "Thomas told me you were asking about me. It must seem strange." She looks at each of them in turn. She has brown eyes except, in the lamplight, the color at the center is wrong. She notices one of the posse noticing and does not change her expression. She says, pleasantly: "You should stay for Sunday service."

### GM Notes
Run Eliza Crane as someone who is not threatening the posse — not yet, and maybe not ever, depending on what she is. The horror here is domestic: a husband who has talked himself into accepting something wrong because he loves what it looks like. Leave ambiguous whether Eliza is a manitou, a Harrowed who has lost all instinct for violence, a spirit wearing a familiar form, or something else the Weird West hasn't catalogued. The Reverend Crane is the lever — he knows something is wrong and has chosen not to know it; the posse's job is to either respect that choice, help him face it, or be there when the choice stops being available. The burial hymn is the tell you can use when the party is ready. Whatever she is, she came back for a reason, and the reason is not cruelty. That doesn't make it safe.
