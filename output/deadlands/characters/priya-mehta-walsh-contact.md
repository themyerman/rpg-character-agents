## **Priya Mehta-Walsh**
*Agency Field Liaison — She crossed the Rockies in a Pullman car with a leather portfolio chained to her wrist, and she's been buying drinks for the wrong sort of people in Dodge City for three days.*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Appears to be** | A Treasury auditor following counterfeit currency west, in need of locals who know the country between here and the Sweetwater valley. |
| **Actually is** | *(revealed only in Truth 2, 3, or 4 below)* |

**Appearance:** A travel-creased grey wool suit cut for a man and altered for her, with ink stains on the right cuff that don't quite wash out. She wears a small enameled pin on her lapel — a federal eagle, but the talons are wrong, gripping a key instead of arrows.

**The Pitch:** *"I'll be direct, because I've watched you long enough to know you prefer it. There's a man at a homestead two days northwest of here — calls himself Elias Crow, though that isn't the name on the warrant. He's been printing notes good enough to pass in three territories, and the Treasury would very much like the plates back. I can't ride in with marshals; the moment a badge crests that ridge he burns everything and runs, and we lose another six months. What I need is four people who can knock on a door and ask to water their horses, and then do what needs doing when he answers. The money is in this envelope. Half now. You don't have to decide tonight, but I'm on the morning train either way."*

**The Job:** Ride to a sod-roof homestead on Cottonwood Creek, northwest of Dodge. Take Elias Crow alive if practical, dead if not. Recover a wooden crate roughly the size of a steamer trunk — contents not to be opened, on pain of forfeiting payment. Deliver crate and (ideally) Crow to a rail siding at Pierceville by Sunday dusk. Six days, all in.

**The Payment:** Four hundred dollars in honest U.S. notes, split however the posse likes — two hundred up front in an oilskin envelope she'll slide across the table. A signed letter of federal goodwill that has gotten more than one wanted man's paper "lost" in the right office. And she'll quietly mention she can find out what happened to a particular missing person, if someone at the table has a name they've been carrying.

**The Truth (GM rolls 1d4 in secret — only one is real):**

1. **Straightforward** — Crow really is a counterfeiter, and a good one. The complication isn't Priya — it's that Crow has a wife and two children at the homestead who don't know what he does in the cellar, and a pair of Pinkerton freelancers tracking the same plates from the other direction who will arrive within hours of the posse and aren't interested in sharing.

2. **One Layer Down** — Crow isn't printing money. He's printing land deeds — forged grants undermining a railroad's right-of-way claim across Cheyenne treaty land. The Treasury wants the plates because the *real* deeds and the forgeries are now indistinguishable, and the only way to settle which families get evicted is to make all of Crow's work disappear. Priya knows this. She considers it tidy.

3. **The Real Story** — Priya isn't Treasury. She's Agency, and the "crate" in the cellar is what they're really after: a working prototype of something Crow stole from a Smith & Robards contract — a device that does *something* to the dead, or to what's left of them. Crow is a former Agency engineer who ran. The currency story is what Priya tells contractors so they don't ask why a federal auditor needs four armed strangers. There is a second team riding parallel; if the posse fails or talks, the second team cleans up the homestead *and* the posse.

4. **The Real West** — There is no counterfeiter. Elias Crow is Elijah Crowfoot, a Lakota engineer who walked out of an Agency black site near Deadwood eighteen months ago carrying evidence — names, dates, ledgers — of what was done there to Reckoning-touched prisoners, most of them Indian. The "homestead" is a safe house run by a Quaker family hiding him. The "crate" is his testimony, bound for a sympathetic congressman in St. Louis. Priya's job is to make sure it never arrives, and to make sure the men who arrive carrying it are strangers nobody will miss. She has done this work for six years. She sleeps fine.

**Why They'd Take It:** Four hundred dollars is four hundred dollars, and the letter is worth more — a posse with paper trouble in any territory east of the Divide can spend that letter exactly once and walk away clean. And Priya does her homework: whatever she offers to "find out" for a specific posse member, she already knows the answer to. She's just deciding whether to share it before the job or after.

**Connection:** **Tomás Beckwith**, the night clerk at the Dodge House, will say she tips in silver dollars and writes her cables in a cipher he's seen Army officers use, "but slower, like she learned it later." He'll add — only if asked twice — that two other men came through last month asking the same kind of questions about the same stretch of country, and neither of them came back through Dodge afterward.
