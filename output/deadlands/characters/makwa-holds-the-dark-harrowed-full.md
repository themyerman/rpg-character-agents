### Edges
- **Harrowed:** He died. He came back. He heals from wounds that would kill an ordinary person, doesn't need to eat or sleep as often as the living do, and has a manitou riding behind his eyes that waits, and watches, and sometimes helps because it is also interested in staying alive. He and the manitou have reached an arrangement. The arrangement is unwritten, untested at its edges, and probably contingent.
- **Arcane Resistance:** The manitou inside him is old enough and strange enough that other supernatural forces — hexes, Shaman workings, Harrowed possession attempts — find him difficult to affect. He doesn't know if this is the manitou protecting its investment or simply what happens when something old enough doesn't want to share.
- **Nerves of Steel:** He has been dead. Pain, as a concept, has a different ceiling for him now. He does not panic in the way living people panic, which is useful in firefights and unsettling in conversation.

### Hindrances
- **The Manitou** (Major): The manitou inside him has wants. It wants violence, yes — but specifically, it wants the violence that serves its purposes, and its purposes overlap with his just enough to be dangerous. When he is severely wounded or psychologically backed against a wall, the Spirit roll to stay in control gets harder. When the manitou takes over, it is efficient, cold, and interested only in the goal it was pursuing when it walked back into his body. He has lost two days at a time. He has found blood on his hands that he put there but does not remember putting there.
- **Outsider** (Minor): He is a dead Ojibwe man walking in the Weird West. Every part of that sentence costs him something in every room he enters. The Harrowed aspect makes whites nervous. The Ojibwe aspect makes everyone else remember something they don't want to remember. The combination produces a silence that he has learned to use and sometimes cannot stand.

### Harrowed Powers
- **Ghost:** He can render himself difficult to perceive — not invisible, but easy to overlook, a feeling in the peripheral vision that redirects attention. The manitou taught him this without being asked; he suspects it's useful to the manitou to sometimes be unseen.
- **Hellfire (Ghost Rock Fever variant):** When the manitou is close to the surface, under extreme stress, he can channel cold rather than heat — a frost that damages from the inside, brief and brutal. He does not use this willingly. The sensation of it is not unpleasant to him, which bothers him more than the power itself.

### Equipment
- **A Springfield Model 1873 Trapdoor carbine, worn clean** — A dead man's rifle. Not his. He took it from the soldier who killed him at Leech Lake — not as a trophy, but because he woke up in the snow beside the body and it was the only weapon within reach. He has carried it ever since. He has not named it because he is still deciding if he should.
- **A beaded bandolier bag, Anishinaabe-made** — His mother's stitching. The contents: dried medicines, a small roll of sinew, a carved wooden figure of a makwa (bear) that his youngest daughter painted red. The figure is the only personal object he has from before. Everything else burned.
- **A blanket coat, trade wool, heavily patched** — Warm enough. The patches are from four different sources and none of them match. He has been on the road for three years and the coat represents all of them.
- **A journal, half-filled, in Anishinaabemowin** — He writes in it during the hours when the manitou is quiet and he is most himself. The journal records what he can remember of Leech Lake, the names of the dead, the names of the living who were scattered. He is finding the living, one by one, and making sure they know they're not forgotten. The manitou does not interfere with this. It has its own reasons.

### Personality
- **Trait:** Speaks less than most people expect and watches more. When he says something, he has thought about it. When he doesn't say something, he has also thought about it.
- **Ideal:** To find his daughter Ziigwan, who was eight years old at Leech Lake and was taken to a government school in the east. She would be eleven now. The school system moves children. He is following the records.
- **Bond:** The journal — specifically the section near the back with a list of forty-one names, living and dead, from Leech Lake and the communities that surrounded it. The living are underlined. He crosses no names off.
- **Flaw:** He is not fully sure that the man walking around in his body is the same man who died on the ice. He thinks so. The manitou, when he has been able to ask it directly in the hours between sleep and waking, says: "Most of you." He does not know what the rest of him is now.

### Connections
- **Ally — Aamoose Trottier, Métis fur trader:** A Métis trader who has been operating the Leech Lake region and surrounding territories for twenty years and who recognized Makwa — and did not run — when he appeared on his doorstep three weeks after a burial he'd attended. Trottier passes messages, shares routes, and occasionally acts as a mail drop for the letters Makwa sends toward the eastern school system. He asks no theological questions. He says: "You were a good man before. Stay that way."
- **Antagonist — Agent Cyrus Mott, Bureau of Indian Affairs:** The BIA agent responsible for the Leech Lake removals, who is currently stationed in St. Paul and who Makwa has been circling for three years. Mott did not pull a trigger at Leech Lake. He signed papers. He knows the records that show where the children were sent. Makwa needs those records. Mott is the kind of man who will not give them willingly, and Makwa is not sure, when he imagines Mott across a room from him, whether the decision he would make in that moment is his or the manitou's.

### Backstory

His name before Leech Lake was Makwa, and he was thirty-one years old: a trapper, a husband, a father of three, fluent in Ojibwe and English and enough French to negotiate with the Métis traders. He guided surveyors through the Minnesota Territory for wages, and he had learned, by then, the particular art of being useful to people who did not consider him a person — doing the work, taking the money, going home to his family. He had calculations about this. He had decided, not without bitterness, that calculations kept his children fed.

October 1876. The Leech Lake Band had been in dispute with the government over lumber rights — trees on their reservation being taken without payment, the formal complaints going nowhere — and when the local Indian Agent ordered a police action to enforce a removal order that the Band disputed, someone fired first, and then everyone fired, and then the snow was red and people who had been eating breakfast were lying in it. Makwa was shot on the ice of the lake. He lay there for most of a day. He was cold the way you stop minding the cold. When he woke up, it was dark, and it was wrong, and there was something behind his eyes that hadn't been there before and was settling in like it planned to stay.

He buried who he could. He could not find Ziigwan. He spent three months in the woods, alone, learning what he was now and making the arrangement with the manitou that has held, more or less, since. Then he started south.

He is looking for his daughter. He is also, in the margins of that search, doing something the manitou approves of: moving through the territories where the Weird West is worst, finding the places where something supernatural is taking advantage of the dispossession and chaos, and making it stop. Not out of heroism. Because the land belongs to the people who live there and have always lived there, and what is being done to that land — and to those people — by the Reckoners and their agents is the same logic as the Indian Agent's removal order wearing a different mask, and he has been done with that logic since he woke up in red snow and decided, one more time, to get up.
