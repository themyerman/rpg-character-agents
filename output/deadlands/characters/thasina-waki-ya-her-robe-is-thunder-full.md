### Edges
- **Arcane Background (Shaman):** She has the Cȟaŋnúŋpa and the songs her uncle taught her. The spirits still answer — but their voices have gone strange since Slim Buttes, like they're shouting from underwater.
- **Danger Sense:** The wind tells her things. A hush in the cottonwoods, a horse's ear flicking the wrong way. She has not been ambushed since she was seventeen.
- **Woodsman:** She can read a track three days old and tell you what the animal was thinking when it left it. The land is still her relative, even this far south.

### Hindrances
- **Death Wish** (Major): She survived Slim Buttes when her sister did not. She has been walking toward something heavy ever since — picking the harder trail, taking point at the worst river crossing, lingering when others run. She tells herself she is being brave. She knows what she is doing.
- **Quirk** (Minor): She will not eat meat she did not see killed, and she speaks a small word of apology over every animal that dies near her — bullet, snake, or stew pot. Whites find it unnerving. So do some Lakȟóta who think she has gone soft in the head.

### Equipment
- **A bone-handled war club (čhaŋȟpí)** — ash haft wrapped in sinew, the stone head bound by her grandmother in 1851. Not decorative. The wood is darker at the grip than anywhere else.
- **A medicine bundle of remedies** — yarrow root, prairie sage, a poultice for bullet wounds that draws faster than anything a frontier doctor carries, and three small twists of something that should not be named to outsiders.
- **A folded buffalo-hide ledger** — pictographs in her own hand recording the names and faces of the twenty-seven dead from Slim Buttes she is carrying home in memory, since their bodies were not returned. She adds to it. She has not finished.
- **A Wanted poster with her name on it** — *"TASHEENA WAKINYAN, Sioux witch-woman, $200 alive, $150 dead, wanted in connection with the disappearance of Lt. Harlan Crowe, 7th Cavalry."* The sketch shows a woman fifteen years older than she is, with the wrong cheekbones. She keeps it folded in her parfleche. She has not decided yet whether it is a trophy or a warning.

### Personality
- **Trait:** Goes very still before she speaks, and speaks quieter the angrier she gets. In a fight she does not shout — she sings, low, under her breath, and the air around her gets colder.
- **Ideal:** To carry the twenty-seven names home to Standing Rock and lay them down properly, so the dead can stop following her.
- **Bond:** Her younger sister Wíŋyaŋ Núŋpa (Two Women), who was thirteen at Slim Buttes and is buried somewhere the army would not tell her.
- **Flaw:** She is not afraid of dying. She is afraid of dying small — of a fever in a stranger's barn, of a stray bullet meant for someone else. So she keeps reaching for something large enough to mean it.

### Connections
- **Ally — Father Ezekiel Moss:** A defrocked Methodist circuit rider who shared a cave with her during a blizzard in the Black Hills and did not try to convert her. He calls what she does prayer. She calls what he does the same. They write letters through a trading post at Fort Pierre.
- **Enemy — Lt. Harlan Crowe, 7th Cavalry:** He gave the order at Slim Buttes. He is not, technically, missing. He is in a place she put him, and what is left of him is still screaming, and the spirits have told her this is not the same as justice. She does not yet know what to do about that.

### Backstory

Before the Reckoning meant anything to her, she was a girl named Tȟašína who could find water in dry country and who her uncle had begun to teach because her hands were steady and her dreams were loud. Standing Rock in the early 1870s was already a hard place — the buffalo thinning, the treaty lines getting redrawn every winter by men in Washington who had never seen the Grand River — but it was home, and the songs still worked, and her sister Wíŋyaŋ Núŋpa followed her everywhere asking too many questions.

September 1876. Slim Buttes. General Crook's column ran down a Miniconjou village fleeing from the Greasy Grass, and Húŋkpapȟa relatives who had ridden to help got caught in it. Tȟašína was nineteen. She watched Lt. Harlan Crowe's troopers shoot her sister in a draw full of chokecherry brush and then ride past the body without looking down. That night she went back for Wíŋyaŋ Núŋpa and could not find her — the army had moved the dead, or the dead had moved themselves, because the land was already going wrong in that part of the Hills. She sat in the draw until dawn and something answered her that was not Wakȟáŋ Tȟáŋka and not the Reckoners either — something older and colder that had been waiting. She made it a promise. It made her one back. Lt. Crowe disappeared from his tent four nights later and the spirits will not tell her, even now, exactly what she did.

She is moving south through Nebraska in the spring of 1877 with twenty-seven names in a buffalo-hide ledger and a wanted poster folded in her parfleche. She is looking for a Pawnee scout named Kúruks Pítku who was at Slim Buttes and who saw where the army took the bodies — he was last heard of working out of Sidney. She wants the burial place. She wants to bring her sister home. And she wants, though she will not say it aloud even to Father Moss, to find something on the road big enough and bad enough that going against it will finally count for the twenty-seven, and for the one.
