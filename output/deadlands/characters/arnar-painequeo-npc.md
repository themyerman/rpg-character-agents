## **Arnar Painequeo**
*Gunfighter working the Kansas–Indian Territory line — a Kiowa man who carries a name half-given by the Icelandic widower who took him in at twelve, and who has spent twenty years deciding what to do with that debt.*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Alignment** | Conflicted |
| **Location** | The back table of any saloon that will seat him — currently a place called the Drovers' Rest, half a day's ride from Fort Sill |

**Appearance:** Lean, mid-thirties, with a tin cross-draw rig worn smooth at the leather. His left hand is wrapped in clean linen even when nothing seems wrong with it — he changes the wrap every morning, and no one has ever seen what's underneath.

**Demeanor:** Quiet to the point of seeming slow. He listens longer than is comfortable before he answers, and when he does, his English is careful, formal, slightly old-fashioned — the English of a Lutheran farmer from the 1850s. People mistake the patience for weakness exactly once.

**Wants:** A job riding shotgun on a freight run east. He says he's tired of the territory. He says it the way a man says he's quitting drink.

**Secret:** He cannot work any mechanism more complicated than a revolver — tools shatter, latches jam, telegraph keys spark in his hand. He's convinced it started the night his foster father's clock stopped at the exact hour the old man died, and he's terrified it means something is still owed. The linen on his hand hides nothing physical. It's a habit. A ritual. The day he stops wrapping it, he believes, is the day whatever's been patient with him stops being patient.

**Hook:** A young Pinkerton with more ambition than sense has been asking around about a Kiowa gunhand who rides with Confederate scrip in his pocket. Arnar would like the question to go away without anyone dying, and he's willing to share what he knows about a recent stagecoach disappearance — knowledge he shouldn't have — in exchange for the party making the Pinkerton lose interest. Or lose the trail. He's not particular about which.

**Equipment:** A pair of Remington revolvers in a cross-draw rig — not a trick, just how he learned. A bottle of good whiskey kept for specific occasions that keep presenting themselves. A long coat that has been shot at more than once and still functions. A coin from a Confederate state, kept for a reason he doesn't explain — and which he sets on the table, heads up, before any conversation he expects to matter.

**Connection:** **Sister Ingrid Halvorsen**, his foster father's daughter by blood, now running a mission school in Lawrence, Kansas. She writes him every month. He has never written back, because the one time he tried, the pen broke in his hand and ink ran across the page in a pattern he refuses to describe. She is the only person alive who would mourn him, and that knowledge is heavier than the guns.
