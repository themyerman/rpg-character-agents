### Edges
- **Arcane Background (Shaman):** Her covenant is with the Sea Ania — the Flower World beneath the desert — and with mountain spirits her grandmother brought north from Cuzco a lifetime ago. She rituals in Yaqui and prays in Quechua, and the spirits here in Apachería listen, but warily. They are not hers.
- **Danger Sense:** The deer-head she wore as an apprentice taught her to hear the world breathing wrong. She wakes the half-second before the gunshot, every time.
- **Woodsman:** Sonoran desert, Sierra Madre highlands, now the dry breaks of west Texas — the land changes but the language of tracks does not.

### Hindrances
- **Wanted** (Major): The Mexican Rurales burned her village at Bácum on Porfirio Díaz's orders and she answered by calling a black rain that drowned a captain and twelve of his men in their saddles. There's a 500-peso bounty on a "Indian witch, scarred left cheek." Bounty hunters have crossed the Rio Bravo more than once.
- **Code of Honor** (Minor): She will not lie to a dying person, will not refuse water to anyone who asks, and will not raise her hand against a child — not even one pointing a rifle at her. She has been shot for the last of these and would be again.

### Equipment
- **A worn Colt 1851 Navy revolver** — taken from a Rural who didn't need it anymore. She cleans it because her teacher taught her that every tool deserves respect, but she has never named it and never will.
- **A folded bundle of papers wrapped in oilcloth** — the burial register of Bácum, with seventy-three names in her own hand. She is carrying it back to anyone who will remember, and to whatever Spirit will witness.
- **Her medicine bundle** — deer hooves, a fragment of obsidian from the Sierra de Bacatete, dried Sonoran tobacco, a knot of her grandmother's hair, and a single dried flower from a place she will not name aloud.
- **A small wooden coyote, badly carved.** Her younger brother made it for her the week before the soldiers came. He was nine. She does not know if he is alive. She has not unwrapped it from its cloth in two years, but she knows by feel exactly where it sits in her pack.

### Personality
- **Trait:** She goes quiet when she's afraid — not frozen, just very still and very polite, the way her grandmother taught her to speak to powerful things. People often mistake this for surrender, once.
- **Ideal:** That the dead deserve to be named, and that someone has to do the naming or they are lost twice.
- **Bond:** Her brother Tetabiate Puma, age nine when she last saw him, possibly conscripted, possibly dead, possibly still in the hills with the resistance. She walks north because going south now means dying before she finds him.
- **Flaw:** The bounty makes her a danger to anyone who shelters her, and she knows it, and she keeps accepting shelter anyway, because the alternative is to stop being a person.

### Connections
- **Ally — Naiche Goyaałé:** A young Chiricahua scout who found her half-dead at a dry wash south of the Chisos and brought her water without asking who she was running from. He speaks four languages and trusts none of them. He owes her nothing and stays anyway.
- **Enemy — Capitán Esteban Coronado:** The Rural officer who survived the black rain at Bácum. His left arm is dead at his side and he tells people a witch did it. He is not wrong. He has crossed into Texas twice looking for her and the second time he brought a Pinkerton.

### Backstory

She was born in Tórim, one of the Eight Sacred Pueblos of the Yaqui along the river of the same name in Sonora. Her mother was Yaqui; her father's mother had walked north from the Andes as a girl in the 1820s, fleeing something she never explained, and the Quechua prayers she taught Ixchel's father were passed down whole. Ixchel grew up dancing the Pascola at Easter and singing to Pachamama at planting, and her grandmother — old by then, blind in one eye — chose her at eleven as the one who would carry both compacts. She trained for nine years. She learned to talk to the Sea Ania the way other girls learned to weave.

The Reckoning, in Sonora, looked like Porfirio Díaz's war of extermination against the Yaqui — and like something older and hungrier riding underneath it, feeding. In the spring of 1874 the Rurales came to Bácum, where she was attending a wedding, and they locked the men in the church and burned it, and they shot the women who ran. Ixchel did not run. She knelt in the ash with her teacher's body across her lap and she called on every Spirit she knew in every language she had, and the sky opened and rained black water for an hour, and Captain Coronado's column drowned standing up in their saddles. She did not know she could do that. She is not sure, even now, what answered her — whether it was the Sea Ania or something wearing its face. Her teacher's bones, what she could gather, are in her bundle.

She fled north because the bounty made Sonora impossible and because a dream told her to. She crossed into Texas at Presidio in the winter of '75 and has been moving since — through Apache country, through the borderlands the Confederacy claims and cannot hold, hunted by Rurales who cross the river illegally and by Pinkertons who don't have to. She is looking for two things, specifically: her brother Tetabiate, who may have been taken by the Mexican army as a labor conscript and shipped to Yucatán, or may be hiding in the Bacatete mountains with the resistance under Cajemé; and she is looking for an answer to a question she will not yet ask out loud — *what did I call down at Bácum, and does it still know my name?*
