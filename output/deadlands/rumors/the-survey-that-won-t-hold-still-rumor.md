## **The Survey That Won't Hold Still**
*A whispered warning passing through railroad workers and surveyors about a Black River Rail Company project in the hills west of Cripple Creek — three men have tried to map the same stretch of land and come back with three contradictory charts.*

### As Heard
> "Don't take that contract if Black River offers it, friend. Theodore Mukherjee — sharp man, twenty years with a transit — surveyed that ridge in March and his chits don't match what the Boggs boy laid down in May, and neither matches what some Pinkerton sketched last week. Compass needles spin, chains come up short, and one fellow says the creek runs *uphill* if you watch it long enough. Whatever's out there don't want to be measured. Walk away."

*Source: Prapas O'Connor, a rod-and-chain man drinking off a bad week at the Silver Hat saloon, repeating what his foreman told him — the foreman who told him not to repeat it.*

### What's Actually True
The land surveys *are* inconsistent, but not because of any natural haunting — at least, not yet. Vihara Lertpanyarak, a junior cartographer at Black River, has been deliberately falsifying every third survey crossing her desk and feeding the "cursed land" rumor through saloon contacts. She's doing it on behalf of a rival concern (Wasatch Rail) that wants Black River to abandon the route. The Pinkerton sketch is real and clean; the other two were doctored. *However* — and this is the Reckoning's joke — the constant fear, paranoia, and three separate ritualized survey attempts have started to attract something. A small manitou has taken interest in the ridge. The land is *becoming* what the rumor claims, slowly. In another month the lies will be true.

### The Danger
A posse that takes the rumor at face value will charge in expecting ghosts and find a tedious corporate espionage case — embarrassing if they make accusations. A posse that dismisses it as hokum and signs on as Black River guards will be on that ridge when the manitou finishes settling in, which means watching their compasses lie and their colleagues wander off cliffs. Worse, exposing Vihara without understanding *why* she did it puts a target on her back — Wasatch doesn't leave loose ends, and she has a younger brother in Denver she's protecting from a gambling debt.

### Hooks
1. Black River offers the posse the survey contract at triple rate. The foreman is sweating. He shows them three maps and asks them to find out which one is right — he doesn't care about the supernatural, he cares about his job.
2. Prapas O'Connor, drunk and guilty, pulls a posse member aside and admits his foreman gave him a list of saloons to spread the warning in. He was paid in Wasatch scrip, which he can't spend locally without questions.
3. On the ridge itself, the posse's own instruments begin disagreeing with each other — a watch runs backward for ninety seconds, then resumes. The manitou is testing whether they're worth haunting.

### GM Notes
Run this as a mystery that pivots: the players think they're hunting a ghost, discover a corporate plot, and then realize the ghost is showing up anyway because the plot summoned it. Escalate by having a survey crew go genuinely missing once the posse commits. Keep Vihara's motive ambiguous — let the players decide whether she's a victim, a villain, or both, and whether her brother in Denver even exists.
