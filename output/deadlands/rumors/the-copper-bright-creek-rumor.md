## **[The Copper-Bright Creek]**
*A piece of saloon gossip about Quinn's Reach mine upstream, the strange color the creek's been running, and the fortune supposedly waiting in the silt for whoever gets there first.*

### As Heard
> "You seen the creek down past the bend? Runs green-gold at sundown now, ever since Callum Quinn brought in that new ghost-rock leaching rig. My cousin panned a handful of grit out of the eddy and walked away with more flake than he'd seen in six months of honest work. Quinn don't even know it's washing downstream — but somebody oughta, 'fore he figures it out and fences the whole valley."

*Source: A washed-up prospector named Roach who's been buying his own drinks for two nights running at the Bitter Mule — telling anyone who'll listen, because he can't work the creek alone and wants partners with guns.*

### What's Actually True
The creek **is** running off-color, and there **is** value in the runoff — but it isn't free gold. Quinn's new process uses a ghost-rock catalyst slurry to strip silver from low-grade ore, and the spent tailings carry both unbound silver flake **and** trace ghost-rock dust into the water. The dust is what's making the creek glow at dusk. Mei Liu, Quinn's Chinese-trained alchemist (and the one who actually designed the process), already knows the tailings are leaking — she's been quietly hiring Pinkertons-for-hire to "discourage" downstream panning while Quinn tries to patent the method. Meanwhile, the Hualapai-Lebanese rancher whose land the creek crosses, Amira Cloudwalker, has noticed her cattle drinking from the creek are turning mean — kicking through fence posts, goring each other. She's not talking yet. She's watching.

### The Danger
Anyone caught panning the creek at night gets a visit from Liu's hired guns — three or four hardcases who won't ask questions, just shoot and roll bodies into the water where they'll wash up looking like another mining accident. Anyone who *does* successfully pan a pouch full of silt is now carrying concentrated ghost-rock dust against their skin — bad dreams within a week, worse luck within two, and the kind of attention from the Reckoners that doesn't wash off. And if the party sides with Quinn against Cloudwalker's land claim, they've made an enemy of someone whose grandmother taught her exactly what to do with cursed water and a long memory.

### Hooks
1. **The Invitation.** Roach corners the party at the Bitter Mule, offers a 60/40 split (his favor) for "muscle on a night job." He has a map. He also has a tail he hasn't noticed — one of Liu's men nursing a beer by the door.
2. **The Rancher's Request.** Amira Cloudwalker rides into town looking for someone who'll believe her about the cattle. She'll pay in cash or beef, and she wants someone to ride the creek *upstream* and find out what Quinn is actually putting in her water.
3. **The Body in the Reeds.** The party finds a dead panner half-submerged a quarter-mile below the mine — pockets full of glittering silt, throat opened ear to ear, and a brass Pinkerton-pattern button clutched in his fist that doesn't match any agency the party's ever heard of.

### GM Notes
Play Liu as competent and reluctant — she'd rather pay people off than kill them, but she will escalate fast if pressed, and her loyalty to Quinn is genuine. Escalate by having the cattle-madness spread to a person: a child, a sheriff, somebody the town can't ignore. Leave ambiguous **what's actually drawn to the ghost-rock dust downstream** — something is following the glow at night, and whether it's a manitou, a hungry spirit Cloudwalker's grandmother named, or just Quinn's hired killers should depend on which thread the party pulls hardest.
