## The Surveyor's Last Camp

*The posse finds an abandoned survey camp in rough country — equipment intact, fire long cold, notes still on the table — and discovers that the surveyor wasn't lost, wasn't ambushed, and wasn't afraid. He was fascinated.*

### Setting
A surveyor's camp in the badlands south of the Black Hills — a canvas tent staked against a rock outcrop, a folding table and a camp stool, a cold fire ring with three days of ash. The camp has not been looted. This is the first strange thing: food tins, two rifles, a good compass, surveying instruments worth real money — all here, untouched. The tent has been here long enough that the canvas is sun-bleached on the south face. The surveyor's name, by the equipment stamps, is **G. FERRIS** — registered with the federal land survey office out of Cheyenne, operating under contract to the Amalgamated Ghost Rock Development Company.

The survey area around the camp is marked with small wooden stakes at regular intervals, the kind used for a geological grid. The grid covers a roughly quarter-mile area of exposed rock shelf. At the center of the grid is a vertical crevice in the rock face, 18 inches wide and 6 feet deep, running straight down. It has been widened recently — the rock edges are fresh-chipped, white against the weathered gray. Someone was trying to make the crevice larger.

### Scene
The folding table holds Ferris's survey notes, three weeks of them. The first two weeks are professional, systematic — soil sample reports, magnetic readings, ghost-rock concentration estimates by depth. Normal survey work. The third week changes. The handwriting is smaller, faster, the margins crowded. The notes stop being about ghost-rock concentrations and start being about a sound. *Day 19: tone from the crevice, low-register, heard it again just after sunset. Not wind. Wind doesn't pulse.* Day 21: *Stayed up to 3 a.m. listening. The pulse is consistent — roughly 40-second interval. It responds to sound. I tapped a rock twice against the shelf and the pulse shifted. I am writing this down because I am a scientist and I should write it down.* Day 23: *Tried the chisel today.* Day 25, the last entry: a rough sketch of the crevice, with measurements noted, and at the bottom of the page in careful block letters: **IT IS WAITING.**

The crevice itself: the rock around the widened portion is warm to the touch. Not sun-warmed — the rock face is in shade. A character who puts their ear to the opening, at the right moment, will hear it: a low, resonant pulse, 40-second intervals, like a heartbeat in stone. If they listen long enough to establish the pattern, the pulse shifts — shortens to 35 seconds — exactly once, and then returns to 40. As if something noticed them.

### Key NPCs
- **G. Ferris (absent)** — His personal effects are here. His horse is gone (the picket line is cut, not untied — the horse left in a hurry). His rifles are here. He was a cautious man; he would not have gone anywhere without a rifle unless he forgot himself. He has not been in this camp for at least 72 hours. The seat of the camp stool is indented to the right, as if he sat here for many hours, leaning toward the crevice.

- **Pinah Morales** — A Tohono O'odham woman in her fifties who appears at the edge of the camp while the posse is investigating, on horseback, watching without speaking. She has been watching this camp from a distance for two days. She will speak with the posse if they speak first and do not draw on her. She knows what is in the crevice. She will not say what it is directly — she will say: "Some things wait a very long time, and do not get less patient." She will say: "Your surveyor went into the hills. He had a lantern. He was singing." She will say: "Don't stay here after dark," and ride away. If the posse follows her, she will answer one more question before she refuses further discussion: if they ask why she's been watching, she says, "Because the last time something woke this, my grandmother had to put it back. I want to know if it woke all the way this time, or just noticed."

- **Corporals Hatch and Vey, U.S. Army Survey Corps** — Arrive at the camp the following morning with orders to secure it pending Ferris's return (he missed a scheduled check-in). They are not hostile to the posse but are technically authorized to hold the camp and all documents as federal property. They haven't read the notes yet. They will read the notes. Their reaction to the notes is the lever you pull.

### Complication
The ghost-rock concentration in this area is genuine and substantial — Ferris's survey data, if recovered and sold to Amalgamated or any other development company, is worth several thousand dollars. The crevice is in the middle of the most promising deposit zone. Any mining operation that opens this area will have to deal with whatever is in the crevice. The development company doesn't know that yet. The Army Survey Corps will note it in their report, categorized as "geological anomaly, requires follow-up," which means it gets folded into a document somewhere and nobody acts on it for two years, by which time whoever buys the mining rights has already started blasting.

Ferris left his rifles and his food and his instruments, and the horse cut its own line to get away, and something in a crevice in the rock adjusted its pattern when a human put an ear to the opening.

### Possible Outcomes
1. **Walk Away** — They don't investigate the crevice further, don't try to find Ferris, leave the camp for the Army. Two months later, a mining crew starts operations in this area. The posse hears about it. Whether they feel the need to go back is up to them.
2. **Find Ferris** — He is in the hills, alive, approximately half a mile from camp in a narrow draw. He has been there for three days. He is not injured. He is sitting with his back against a rock, facing west, with the look of a man who heard something that rearranged his understanding of the world and hasn't finished rearranging himself around it. He will say, when they find him: "It showed me what it was." He will not say what it was. He will say: "It's been there since before people were here. It's been patient. It just wanted —" He stops. He says: "I don't think it means harm." He believes this. Whether he's right is the GM's decision and should not be made lightly.
3. **Open the Crevice Further** — The tone changes when they start working on the rock. It doesn't stop — it changes register, becoming lower, slower, the intervals elongating. The rock around the crevice develops a faint luminescence, pale blue, that fades when they stop working. Whatever is in there is not trying to stop them. It is waiting. The question of what is waiting, and what it will do when the opening is large enough, is the heart of the scenario — and Pinah Morales's grandmother had to put it back once, which means there's a way, and means it can be done, and means someone paid a cost to do it the first time.

### GM Notes
Run this as a mystery that respects both registers: the rational (Ferris's survey data, the Army's legal claim, the development company's interest) and the Weird West (whatever is in the crevice, Morales's grandmother, the pulse that responds). The encounter earns its horror by being ambiguous — is this a manitou, an ancient spirit, a geological phenomenon that the Reckoners have learned to use, or something that predates the Reckoning entirely? Leave it unresolved unless the party commits to the deep investigation. Ferris is alive and can be a recurring character — a former methodical man who has been changed by contact with something he can't categorize, useful and unreliable in equal measure. The development company is the clock: when the dynamite crews arrive, the posse will have to decide whether they warned anyone, and whether the warning would have mattered.
