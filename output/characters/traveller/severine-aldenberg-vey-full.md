### Career History

**Term 1 (age 18–22):** Recruited at nineteen — quietly, at a function, by a woman who had known her mother in another life. The Service wanted what she already was: a diplomat's daughter who'd been listening at the right doors since she could walk. Her first posting was a soft one, a cultural attaché cover in the Spinward Marches, and she handled it so deftly that she came home with a commendation, a promotion to Field Agent, and the lifelong loyalty of a station chief named Daugherty who had watched her turn a routine asset cultivation into a windfall.

**Term 2 (age 22–26):** Sent deeper. A long-cover operation on a corporate world ended badly — a courier they were running burned them both and a clean-room extraction turned into a service-tunnel crawl in formal evening wear. She and her case officer, Ilan Marek, came out alive and on schedule, which the Service called a success and which neither of them ever called anything at all. No promotion. She did, however, learn that she could lie to a man with a gun on her ribs without her pulse changing.

**Term 3 (age 26–30):** The bad years. A superior — a man named Cortez whose career she had, without intending to, eclipsed at a regional review — handed her an operation that had been engineered to fail and to fail *publicly*. She got out. The asset did not. She was reprimanded, formally, in writing, by people who privately apologized in person. Cortez was reassigned upward. She learned the most important lesson of her life that year, and she has never written it down.

**Term 4 (age 30–34):** The Service, embarrassed by what it had done to her, made it up to her the way the Service does — by giving her the door to the specialist track. Tradecraft, signals, close-quarters work, the blade course at Regina. She came out the other end thinner, quieter, more lethal, and a Special Agent. Her instructors filed reports using words like *exceptional* and *unsettling.*

**Term 5 (age 34–38):** Two years as a roving Field Supervisor running her own small string of agents through the Sword Worlds margins. She was good at it — perhaps too good; the Service began floating her name for a directorate posting on Capital. She felt the first grey hair at thirty-six, the first stiffness in the knife-hand at thirty-seven, and at thirty-eight she filed her papers, took her pension, and walked out the door before they could put her behind a desk. Daugherty, now retired himself, sent her a single bottle of Khauran ice-wine and a note that read: *Good. Now disappear properly.*

### Backstory

She was raised in the sealed embassy quarter of Khaurys, where her parents — career diplomats, both of them — taught her four languages, three forks, and the survival lesson that on a world whose air will eat you, every door is a checkpoint and every smile is a negotiation. The wound is Cortez, and what she did to keep the asset he sacrificed from being saved — not what he did, what *she* did, the calculation she made in the seventeen seconds she had to make it, and the fact that she would make the same call again. She is thirty-eight, beautiful in a way that makes rooms recalibrate, officially retired, drifting between worlds on her own dwindling High Passage, and looking — without ever quite saying so — for a piece of work small enough to be honest and large enough to be worth the knife.
