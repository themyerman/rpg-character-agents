## **Yutani Survey Relay VK-7**
*A deep-survey relay station that stopped answering automated pings fourteen months ago and is now answering something else.*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Type** | Unmanned deep-survey relay station, Weyland-Yutani Survey Division |
| **Condition** | Power nominal, comms partially active, life-support running — no crew on manifest |

### Atmosphere
VK-7 smells wrong before the airlock finishes cycling — recycled air with too much moisture in it, and underneath that something organic, faintly sweet, the smell of something biological that has been working in a contained space for a long time. The lighting is on emergency amber, which is either a fault state or a protocol. The deck is clean, which is the first thing that will seem strange: no dust, no debris, nothing knocked over. Whatever has been using this station has been keeping it tidy, or something about the station's processes has kept it tidy, and those are not the same thing.

The comms suite is active. It is transmitting. The transmission is not on any scheduled W-Y survey relay frequency, and the content — pulled on first contact — appears to be sensor logs: atmospheric readings, motion data, spectrographic results. Automated. Systematic. As if the station has been conducting a survey of its own interior for the past fourteen months.

### Notable NPCs
- **Station Log AI "VK-7-CONTROL"** — The facility's automated management system, which is functional, responsive to queries, and very precise about what it will and will not answer. It reports "nominal operations," lists zero personnel aboard, and confirms all equipment is functioning within specification. It will not confirm what initiated the 14-month communications blackout. It will answer the question "is there anything aboard I should be aware of?" with the sentence: "All systems are operating as intended."
- **Dr. Meena Okonkwo** *(voice only, recording)* — The lead survey technician whose last log entry, dated fourteen months and three days ago, is audio only. She sounds calm. She describes a "contact event" in the sub-level access corridor. She says, in a tone that is curious rather than alarmed, that the structural survey drone has returned from the lower level with something on its sampling pad that she doesn't recognize. She says she's going to go take a look. The recording ends with the sound of a pressure door cycling, and then, faint, something that might be footsteps, or might be something else moving through a corridor that doesn't have anything in it that should be moving.
- **The Drone (designation VK-7-SD-3)** — Still operational, still in its dock in the lower access level. Its sampling pad was cleaned. Its optical sensors, if reviewed, contain 847 hours of footage from the lower sub-level that VK-7-CONTROL will only release under a personnel authorization code belonging to Dr. Okonkwo.

### The Situation
VK-7 was a three-person automated survey relay; the crew rotated out eighteen months ago and were not replaced, as W-Y Survey Division reclassified the station as unmanned following a budget review. Dr. Okonkwo, Dr. Lee Chamberlain, and tech specialist Rafał Wroński should have departed on the relief shuttle. The manifest says they did. The station says they did. The personnel records say they did.

The lower sub-level access corridor — a maintenance passage running beneath the primary deck, used for duct access and equipment servicing — has been sealed from the inside for fourteen months. The seal is not a lock. It is biological material, dark, dry, leathery, filling the door frame in a way that looks, at first, like damage from a seal failure. It is not a seal failure. Whatever is in the lower corridor has been there long enough for the material to cure to the consistency of old rubber, and whatever produced it is not in the W-Y biological survey database, which the station's AI will helpfully confirm.

The station's primary transmitter is pointed at a W-Y fleet coordination frequency. The transmission content — the 14 months of interior sensor logs — has been received and archived somewhere by someone. Nobody has come.

### Hooks
1. VK-7-CONTROL offers full station access in exchange for personnel authorization from Dr. Okonkwo. The party has her personnel ID on the standard crew manifest they were given for this sector. Using it accesses the drone footage — and pings a W-Y Special Acquisitions flag that has been sitting on Okonkwo's ID for fourteen months, waiting for someone to use it.
2. The biological seal on the lower corridor door is intact but thin at the upper left corner. Something was trying to get through from the other side, recently — the material is cracked there, and the cracks are fresh. The station sensors show no movement. The station sensors cover every room except the lower sub-level.
3. The survey drone's optical storage is accessible via a maintenance interface that bypasses VK-7-CONTROL entirely. Pulling the footage requires 20 minutes with a Comtech roll. What it shows — the lower sub-level, unedited, 847 hours — is the GM's call. The last 40 hours of footage are blanked. Not corrupted. Blanked, as if the drone's recording was deliberately disabled, from the inside.

### GM Notes
Run VK-7 as a place where the horror is in the evidence trail, not the immediate threat. The station itself is not currently dangerous — whatever happened here is contained, or was, until the party arrived. Let the players find the pieces in any order: the recording, the sealed door, the drone footage, the transmitter, the personnel manifest that shows three people who left. Escalate by having W-Y Special Acquisitions contact the party — politely, promptly, professionally — to inquire about their current location and estimated departure time. Leave ambiguous whether Okonkwo, Chamberlain, and Wroński are somewhere in the lower sub-level, somewhere else entirely, or somewhere the station's manifest is accurate about in a way the party hasn't yet understood.
