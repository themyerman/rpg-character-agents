## The Last Authorized Transmission

*The party receives a distress signal from a W-Y science team on a survey moon — but when they arrive, the signal is still broadcasting, the equipment is undamaged, and the only thing missing is the science team.*

### Setting
Survey Moon LV-1247, a tidally locked rock orbiting a gas giant in the outer survey corridor — cold, low-gravity, a thin nitrogen atmosphere that makes the horizon shimmer. The W-Y survey installation is a pre-fab dome complex, three modules connected by pressurized corridors: a habitat block, a geological survey lab, and a specimen storage unit. The exterior lights are on. The power grid is fully operational. The distress signal is broadcasting on the standard W-Y emergency channel, automated, looping a message recorded by the team leader forty-eight hours ago: *"Survey team Harkov-9 requesting immediate assistance, survey moon LV-1247. We have a containment issue with specimen storage. Please respond. Please."* The message ends. It loops.

The docking airlock cycles normally. The habitat block is clean. The bunks are made. The coffee in the galley is cold.

### Scene
The installation has been occupied until recently — within the last twelve to twenty-four hours, based on the ambient temperature of the coffee, the position of chairs, the half-completed survey logs on the terminal. Four people were here. Three survey technicians and a W-Y liaison named **Dr. Harlow Mistry**, who recorded the distress signal and whose ID code is on the terminal log as the last person to access the specimen storage unit, forty-seven hours ago. The terminal, if accessed, shows a clean activity log with one notable gap: a four-hour window, forty-eight to forty-four hours ago, where nothing was recorded and nothing was accessed — not during a system restart, not during a power fault, but because the terminal's logging function was manually disabled and then manually re-enabled.

Specimen storage is locked. The lock is standard W-Y survey kit — requires a biometric and an access code. Mistry's biometric is logged as the last access. His code is in the team's standard documentation package. The door opens.

### Key NPCs
- **Dr. Harlow Mistry** — W-Y Liaison, survey team lead, age 41. The recorded distress message is his voice: controlled, exhausted, a man choosing words carefully. He knew he was recording something that might be the last record of what happened here. He chose his words accordingly, which means he left things out as deliberately as he put things in. The things he left out are in specimen storage.

- **Technician Adaeze Obiechina** *(voice only, notes)* — Left handwritten observation notes in the geological survey lab, dated over the past two weeks. They document standard survey work — sample collection, analysis, mapping. The last entry is three days ago: "Day 18 — Mistry pulled the outer sector samples from the standard queue. Says Special Research wants them processed separately. I asked why. He said to just do the standard queue." The note ends. Below it, in different handwriting — neater, more deliberate — a single line in pencil: *"Do not let them leave the dome."* It is not Obiechina's handwriting.

- **Whatever is in Specimen Storage Unit 3** — Mistry's distress signal said "containment issue." The storage unit holds sixteen sample cases, a cryogenic specimen locker (standard W-Y survey kit, rated for small bioform containment, currently reading "OCCUPIED: 1"), and a personal case that was not listed on the team's manifest. The personal case is Mistry's. It is locked by a different code than his standard ID. Whatever he put in it, he locked separately.

### Complication
The distress signal is automated — it was set up by Mistry to broadcast if he didn't personally cancel it within 24 hours. He did not cancel it, which means either he couldn't or he didn't want to, and those are not the same situation. But the signal is also drawing a second vessel: a W-Y Special Research response team, currently six hours out, who acknowledged the signal before the party did and whose acknowledgment was addressed not to Survey Team Harkov-9 but to *"the recovery of Special Research assets, LV-1247."* They have not contacted the party. They know the party is here — their approach vector puts them on an intercept with the installation's dock — and they have not said anything. They are six hours away. The specimen locker in storage unit 3 reads: OCCUPIED.

### Possible Outcomes
1. **The Clean Exit** — The party takes whatever is in specimen storage, seals the installation, and clears the docking window before the W-Y response team arrives. They are now in possession of W-Y Special Research assets and a personal locked case belonging to a missing man, and the response team will want to know where they're going. So will Mistry, if Mistry is alive.
2. **The Handoff** — The party stays, cooperates with the W-Y response team, turns over the installation. The response team is polite, efficient, and produces paperwork for everything. One of the four missing survey team members is returned — Mistry, alive, debriefed, cleared, with a composure that does not match the voice on the distress recording. He does not make eye contact with the specimen locker. The party is thanked and cleared to depart. Nobody says where the other three are.
3. **Open the Locker** — Whatever is in specimen storage unit 3 is the pivot. Open it and the situation becomes irreversible. The party will know what W-Y is moving, what Mistry's four-hour log gap was spent doing, and what the pencil note in Obiechina's handwriting was meant to prevent. They will also be dealing with it in a three-module dome on a moon where the nearest help is six hours out and coming for the asset, not for them.

### GM Notes
*What's in the locker and where the survey team went are the same answer, but the party should reach the first question before the second. Mistry's personal case contains a field record — handwritten, 40 pages — of everything he documented and did not include in his official logs, going back to the day Special Research sent him the "outer sector processing" instruction. He was not a whistleblower. He was a loyal company man who kept records because loyal company men keep records, and the records document a progression he didn't fully understand until it was too late to stop. The three missing technicians are in the dome. They are not in the rooms the party has seen. Survey installations have a sub-floor maintenance space — environmental systems, waste processing, cabling — accessed by a floor panel in the habitat block that is, currently, held down by a sample case that was placed on top of it and not moved. Whether what is in the sub-floor is a containment measure, a hiding place, or something else is the question the party will be answering when the W-Y response team docks.*
