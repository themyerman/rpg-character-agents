## **The Beacon Was Already Off**
*Fires during approach to a standard W-Y survey waystation that is broadcasting a normal arrival beacon — until it stops, mid-approach, for eleven seconds, then resumes as if nothing happened.*

### When It Fires
The crew is six hours from a routine W-Y survey relay station — a standard refuel-and-message-drop waypoint, staffed by a rotating crew of two, unremarkable in every documented particular. The station's arrival beacon has been broadcasting on schedule. Nineteen minutes into final approach, the beacon cuts. Eleven seconds of silence. Then it resumes, normal frequency, normal ID code, normal automated welcome message. The nav computer logs the interruption as a "signal anomaly" and suggests it was atmospheric interference. There is no atmosphere.

### What Happens
The station is broadcasting normally. The approach window is open, the docking protocols respond correctly, and the station AI — which runs the automated systems when crew are off-shift — confirms two personnel aboard in their scheduled sleep rotation. Everything checks out.

What doesn't check out: the beacon interruption was eleven seconds long. Standard W-Y station beacons have a hardware failsafe that resumes broadcast within 0.3 seconds of any interruption. An eleven-second gap means the beacon was not interrupted by equipment failure — it was deliberately disabled, held for eleven seconds, and then re-enabled. Someone on that station turned the beacon off. Then turned it back on. During the window when a ship was in approach range.

### The Party's Position
They are committed to the approach — changing course now costs twelve hours of fuel and a route correction that will trigger an automatic W-Y logistics inquiry about the deviation. Everything about the station reads normal. The only anomaly is a beacon gap that the nav computer has already attributed to atmospheric interference and logged as resolved. Nobody has to do anything about this. They can dock, refuel, drop their mail packet, and leave in four hours.

### Possible Responses
1. **Dock normally, say nothing about the beacon.** Clean, fast, efficient. But the moment they're aboard the station, they're either going to meet the two crew members — and have to decide what to say, and whether to ask — or they're going to meet the station AI and the particular silence of a facility where the sleeping crew are sleeping very deeply.
2. **Contact the station on comms before docking.** The station AI will answer and route them to a crew channel. One of the two crew members will come on: professional, awake despite the schedule, slightly too prompt. They will say everything correct. The party's job is to listen to the tone under the correct things.
3. **Request a visual through the docking camera before completing approach.** The station will comply — the docking camera image is clean, the station's exterior is undamaged, the docking bay is properly lit. In the lower left corner of the image, barely visible against the station hull, there is a handprint in something dark, palm down, fingers spread, at a height that suggests the person who made it was trying to reach the exterior access panel.

### Complications
- The eleven-second beacon gap occurred at 03:17 station time, which is mid-sleep rotation. The crew manifest shows both personnel as having checked in for their sleep shift at 23:00. Somebody was awake at 03:17 and at the beacon controls. This is not in itself unusual — sleep schedules slip on long rotations. What is unusual is that the beacon controls are in the communication room, and the communication room logs show no entry for either crew member since 22:40 the previous day.
- The station's scheduled message-drop queue includes a packet for W-Y Special Research flagged as "PRIORITY — TIME SENSITIVE." It was queued for transmission 03:18 — one minute after the beacon resumed. It did not transmit. It is still in queue, held by a manual transmission lock that requires a physical key at the communications console. The key is not in the console.
- The station AI, if asked directly whether both crew members are in good health, will answer: "All station personnel are currently within documented physiological parameters." It will not answer the follow-up question about whether both crew members are mobile and conscious, because no one thought to program a direct answer to that specific question.

### GM Notes
Run the eleven-second gap as the hook that makes the players decide how much they want to know before they commit to docking. The reveal — what happened on the station during that eleven seconds, and where the key is, and what's in the W-Y Special Research packet — should be calibrated to your campaign's horror register. At the quiet end: both crew members are fine, one of them made an error and panicked and is now hoping nobody noticed; the handprint is old, the packet is routine, the gap is coincidence. At the other end: one of them made the gap, held the beacon off long enough to leave the handprint and hide the key, and is now in a sleep cycle they will not wake from on their own. The story the party gets is the one they choose to look for.
