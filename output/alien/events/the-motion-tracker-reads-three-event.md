## **The Motion Tracker Reads Three**
*Fires mid-transit, during a quiet watch rotation, when someone doing a routine equipment check points a motion tracker at a section of the ship that should be empty.*

### When It Fires
The crew has been underway for three weeks. Nothing has gone wrong. The watch rotation has become comfortable, the predictable rhythm of transit, and tonight's watch — whoever drew the short rotation — is doing a standard perimeter sweep of the aft section with a motion tracker, the kind of check that gets logged and forgotten. The aft section is uninhabited crew storage and venting shafts. The motion tracker is reading three contacts.

### What Happens
Two of the contacts are consistent with the crew members currently on the aft deck — the character with the tracker, and whoever is working nearby. The third contact is in the ventilation shaft access above cargo storage bay two. It is 1.4 meters off the deck in a shaft that runs the length of the aft section — a shaft that is 60 centimeters in diameter, which is large enough, technically, for a person to move through prone, though it would be a painful transit. The contact is not moving. It is stationary, oriented toward the cargo bay below it, and it has been there long enough that the shaft access panel in bay two is cool to the touch in a way that suggests the panel has been open recently and the air has had time to equalize.

The contact does not respond to sound. It does not move when the ventilation system cycles. The tracker reading is solid, consistent, and approximately the mass of a large person or a very large animal.

### The Party's Position
They are three weeks from the nearest waystation, with a crew of some number, and something is in the ventilation system above the cargo bay. They don't know how it got there, how long it's been there, or what it is — a stowaway, a crew member in distress, something that came with the cargo, or a tracker malfunction at the worst possible time. They also don't know if it knows they've found it.

### Possible Responses
1. **Open the access panel from below.** The most direct answer — but "direct" in a vent shaft means whatever's inside has nowhere to go except toward the sound of the opening panel, and moving toward something in a confined space is not ideal when you don't know what the something is.
2. **Seal the shaft and wait.** Sustainable for as long as the ventilation system doesn't need that run — which is between six and twenty-two hours, depending on the environmental load. After that, the ship's systems will attempt to re-open the sealed vents automatically, and the party will get a maintenance alert they'll have to manually override.
3. **Access from the far end — crawl through.** The shaft has two access points: bay two below it, and the environmental control room at the forward end of the section, 40 meters away. Someone could enter at the forward end and move toward the contact. This is the slow, enclosed, confined-space approach, in the dark, toward a mass contact that isn't moving. The party will have to decide who goes.

### Complications
- The motion tracker's battery, which was fully charged at the start of the watch, is now reading 12%. This is not possible — the unit should have 6+ hours of charge. Whatever is generating the contact is drawing power from somewhere.
- Cargo bay two holds, among other things, whatever was in the sealed secondary consignment. The vent shaft contact is directly above the secondary consignment's position on the bay floor. The secondary consignment's temperature sensor — a passive monitor, no transmitter — is reading 2.3 degrees Celsius higher than it was at the last check six hours ago.
- The contact moves, once, while the party is deciding: 40 centimeters, toward the access panel, then stops. Deliberate. As if something in the shaft is waiting to see what they do next.

### GM Notes
Run this as a pressure scene where the horror is in the indeterminacy — the party cannot know what they're dealing with without looking, and looking commits them. Hold off on revealing whether the contact is a person, an animal, or something else for as long as the scene can sustain the tension; the answer changes the scene's nature completely and should be earned. If you want to escalate into a containment scenario, make it something that got in with the cargo. If you want a human story, make it a stowaway with a reason for being there that complicates everything. If you want the worst version: the contact is crew. Someone is in that shaft who has been there for a long time, and they are alive, and when the party opens the panel they will say something that makes sense of everything the ship has been carrying, and it will be worse than a bioform.
