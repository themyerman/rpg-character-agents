## **Mahpee Thunderbird**
*Independent logistics broker — a soft-spoken middleman with a thin smile who insists he's "just the messenger."*

| | |
|---|---|
| **Appears to be** | A freelance freight broker working a contract on behalf of a research consortium he won't name |
| **Actually is** | *(revealed only in Truth 2, 3, or 4 below)* |

**Appearance:** A wiry man in his late forties wearing a tailored civilian jacket two seasons out of fashion, the kind a person buys when they've stopped caring about appearances but still need to look like they do. His left thumb is missing the nail entirely — regrown skin, smooth and pink, like something was pulled out from under it. He carries a slim datapad in a hardshell case that he never sets down, not even to drink.

**The Pitch:** *"I'm going to be straight with you, because I respect your time. I have a sealed container — single unit, two meters by one, refrigerated, self-contained power for forty days. It needs to go from Gateway Station to a private research outpost on LV-872. The client wants it off the standard lanes, which means you'd be taking the Hadley corridor through the Veil. I'm not going to insult you by pretending that's a normal route. That's why the number is what it is. You don't open the container. You don't scan the container. You deliver it sealed, you get paid, you go home. I've run this same job four times with four different crews and every one of them is still flying. That should tell you what you need to know."*

**The Job:** Transport one sealed cryo-container from Gateway Station to the Trellis-9 research outpost on LV-872, via the Hadley corridor (a slow, unmonitored transit route through the Veil Nebula adding roughly eleven days to standard transit). Container must remain sealed, unscanned, and within a 2°C operating window throughout. Delivery window: 26 days from acceptance. Contact will not be aboard — he hands off the container at Gateway and meets the crew on arrival.

**The Payment:** 18,000 credits on delivery, 4,000 up-front as fuel and provisioning advance. A second sealed envelope contains a Weyland-Yutani-issued transit waiver good for one unrestricted hyperspace jump through controlled space — the kind of document that, in the right circumstances, is worth more than the credits.

**The Truth (GM rolls 1d4 in secret — only one is real):**

1. **Straightforward** — The container holds a stabilized biological sample for legitimate (if grey-market) research. Mahpee is exactly what he says he is. The complication is the Hadley corridor itself: a derelict ore-hauler has been broadcasting a faint distress loop in the Veil for the last six weeks, and ICC regulations technically require any vessel passing within range to investigate. The crew on that hauler did investigate something, three months ago. They didn't all come back inside.

2. **One Layer Down** — The container's temperature differential has been climbing, slowly, for eighteen hours before handoff — Mahpee knows but considers it "within tolerance per the client's spec sheet." The contents are a viable specimen in extended dormancy, not a stabilized sample. Bumps, hull stress, or a thermal drift past the window will wake it. Mahpee was told the container could handle "normal transit shocks." He was not told what normal meant to the people who packed it.

3. **The Real Story** — Mahpee runs courier work for a faction inside a mid-tier biotech firm (Lasalle-Genodyne) that is hemorrhaging assets to a hostile Weyland-Yutani acquisition. The container is one of three identical decoys being routed simultaneously by three different crews; the real specimen is on a fourth ship that left two days ago. The crew is bait — visible enough to draw W-Y interdiction attention away from the actual cargo. Mahpee knows this. He doesn't think W-Y will shoot. He could be wrong.

4. **The Company Knew** — Mahpee's "research consortium" is a Weyland-Yutani shell. The container holds a live specimen the Company wants delivered to LV-872's Trellis-9 — a black-budget facility that has lost contact with its previous three shipments and three previous crews. The Hadley corridor routing is not for discretion; it's because the corridor's lack of traffic means no one will hear the distress call when the container's internal stasis lapses on day nineteen of the transit. Mahpee has done this job four times. He has met four different crews at Gateway. He has never actually met any of them on arrival. The transit waiver is real. The delivery bonus is governed by Clause 17(d): *"Payment void in the event of cargo compromise, crew incapacitation, or failure to complete transit under original seal."*

**Why They'd Take It:** The 4,000 up-front clears two months of berthing fees and a fuel top-off with margin to spare. The transit waiver alone solves a problem any crew running grey cargo eventually has. And eleven extra days in the Veil is eleven days of nobody asking where you've been — which, for a crew with anything in their own hold they'd rather not declare, is its own kind of payment.

**Connection:** *Yolanda Briggs*, dockmaster at Gateway Station's C-ring, has signed off on Mahpee's manifests six times in the last two years. Ask her about him and she'll shrug: *"Pays his fees in hard credits, never asks for receipts, never argues a weight discrepancy. Polite man. I noticed he started using a different forwarding address about a year back — same name on the paperwork, but the routing changed. Didn't seem like my business to ask. Still doesn't."*
