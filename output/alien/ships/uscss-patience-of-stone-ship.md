### Description
She's ugly in the way of all working ships — not elegantly ugly, but functionally ugly, the ugliness of a thing built to do a job and patched and re-patched to keep doing it. The primary hull is a long gray rectangle with the W-Y sunburst logo on the aft port side, partially overpainted where a previous operator's livery was removed and the new coat didn't quite cover. The cargo hold is her mass and her purpose: three deck-spanning bays, crane-equipped, with tie-down ratings that suggest she was once spec'd for military transport contracts she no longer runs.

Inside, the *Patience of Stone* smells of recycled air and the specific industrial cleaner that W-Y uses in all its deep-transit vessels — a smell that crewmembers who've been on W-Y hulls for more than a year stop noticing and never quite lose. The corridors are standard 1.8-meter clearance, lit by overhead fluorescents in two shades of white (the aft section runs a yellower light; a bulb array was replaced twelve months ago with the wrong spec and nobody has requisitioned the right ones). The galley is large for a vessel this size — a previous refit expanded it, removing what was a secondary medical bay — and is the closest thing to a social center the ship has.

### History
The *Patience of Stone* was laid down at the Weyland-Yutani Tinian yards eleven years ago for long-haul commercial contract work in the outer survey corridor. Her first five years were unremarkable: survey supply runs, colonial transport, equipment repositioning. She was acquired by an independent operator — Captain Miriam Tolosa — in her sixth year, who ran her on the Thedus-to-Rim circuit for three years before a cargo dispute with a Seegson affiliate and a mechanical failure at Waypoint Kovacs left her impounded.

W-Y Special Transit Division reacquired her at auction eighteen months ago at a fraction of her assessed value, which is the kind of acquisition that only makes sense if you know what the W-Y Special Transit Division actually uses "retired commercial assets" for. She was refitted — cargo security systems upgraded, hypersleep vault expanded by two pods, the secondary medical bay converted to galley space — and put back on commercial manifest under a new operator agreement. Her current operating captain, **Elan Desroches**, is sixteen months into a two-year contract. He has asked, twice, why a commercial hauler needed upgraded cargo security. Both times the question was answered with paperwork.

### Quirks
- **The Galley is Too Big:** The expanded galley feels off — too much space for the crew complement, with a counter and storage that make sense if you're provisioning for twelve and not seven. The aft cabinet runs cold. Nobody put perishables back there after the first month.
- **Bay Three Won't Seal Fully:** The inner cargo door for bay three has a seal failure on the lower right corner — a 3mm gap that the system flags as within acceptable tolerance. It is within acceptable tolerance. It also means that bay three runs slightly colder than the others, and that sounds from bay three carry into the corridor in a way that sounds from one and two don't.
- **The Previous Captain's Protocol Log:** Tolosa's operational protocols are still loaded in the ship's management system — her preferred approach vectors, her maintenance scheduling preferences, her emergency contact hierarchy. They've been overlaid by new protocols, not replaced, so occasionally the system defaults to her preferences when a new instruction doesn't specify. Her emergency contact is listed as a W-Y logistics officer who left the company four years ago. Nobody has updated it.
- **She Runs Quieter After 0300:** The automated maintenance cycle runs from 0100 to 0400 ship time, and for that window, the environmental fans cycle down to minimum-draw mode. The ship gets very quiet. In that quiet, crewmembers in aft-section bunks can hear the structural flex of the hull under thermal variation — a low, irregular sound that the mechanics will tell you is completely normal and that new crew universally find unsettling.
- **Bay Two Crane Responds to Two Operators:** The cargo crane in bay two will respond to standard operator commands from the crew roster. It will also respond, for reasons no maintenance review has been able to identify, to operator credential 7-J-ALPHA, which is not on any crew manifest and which appears nowhere in the ship's registered user database. Commands entered under 7-J-ALPHA have never done anything harmful. They have moved cargo by amounts that were not requested. Twice.

### Crew Roles
- **Captain** — Inherits an operator agreement with clauses about "Special Transit consignments" that W-Y can invoke at any time, and a ship that was refitted for reasons that were never fully explained. The job requires being comfortable with ambiguity or very good at performing comfort.
- **Pilot / Navigator** — The *Patience* is not a responsive ship; she commits to a heading and changes it slowly. The pilot's value is in reading the route, not the controls. She rewards patience, which may be why she's named what she is.
- **Chief Engineer** — Eleven years of systems, three major refits, and one operator change mean the *Patience of Stone*'s engineering record is a palimpsest. The engineer's job is knowing which systems they actually understand and which ones they're managing on institutional inertia.
- **Cargo Master** — Three bays, multiple consignment types, and an upgraded security system that requires a separate authorization for bay three access. The cargo master controls bay access logs. What that means depends on who's watching.
- **Medical Officer / Synthetic** — The ship has a synthetic, model designation not current-generation, running medical and emergency protocols. She has been on this ship for four of its eleven years. She has opinions about the bay three security upgrade that she will share if asked in the right way.

### Current Situation
The *Patience of Stone* is docked at Waypoint Kovacs, where she has been for six days awaiting a cargo consignment from a W-Y Special Research contractor. The consignment has been delayed — the contact has rescheduled three times without explanation — and Captain Desroches has been told only that the consignment is "time-sensitive biological research materials requiring standard Class-5 handling." The delay has cost him six days of berthing fees. He has started asking questions he knows won't be answered, which is how he found out that the previous delay request came not from the contractor but from a W-Y Special Acquisitions authorization code. Special Acquisitions is not Special Research. The ship's synthetic, when Desroches asked her whether she knew what was in the consignment, said: "I have not been provided with that information, Captain." She said it looking at the galley floor. He has never seen her do that before.

### GM Notes
*The consignment is not biological research materials. It is a live specimen in a transit container, being moved under the cover of a commercial manifest that can survive a standard customs review. The bay three security upgrade was installed specifically for this shipment — the seal gap in bay three is a deliberate feature, not a fault, because the specimen's environmental requirements mean that perfect sealing would raise internal bay temperature to a flaggable level. The synthetic knows something about the cargo she was instructed to not share with the crew. She was not instructed to deceive the crew — W-Y did not instruct her to say it was biological research materials; Desroches' W-Y contact did that independently. The synthetic, operating within her instruction parameters, has been as precise as those parameters allow. The crane in bay two has been responding to operator credential 7-J-ALPHA since the refit. Tolosa, the previous captain, had 7-J-ALPHA as her emergency override. She is not dead. She knows what W-Y is using her ship for. She has been watching.*
