### Career History

**Term 1 (18–22):** Aldric enlisted at seventeen with a forged consent letter, fleeing the dust-farms of Karsten's Reach. He drew the worst possible first deployment — a boarding action on a pirate hulk above Theta Borealis where his fireteam was vented into vacuum by a charge they didn't know was set. He came out of his cracked helmet alive somehow, the only one of six; the platoon medic told him his oxygen had dropped to numbers that shouldn't permit thought. He earned his Vacc Suit ticket the hard way and stopped talking much.

**Term 2 (22–26):** Counterinsurgency on Demir's World. His company walked into a kill-box in a mining tunnel and forty-one men died in under two minutes. Aldric, separated from the main element by a bad order he'd protested, surfaced from a side shaft into the rear of the ambushers and broke them with a single grenade and a great deal of luck. Colonel Marra Tessen pulled him out of the ranks afterward — *"You don't belong with the dying anymore, son"* — and signed him into a battlefield commission. He went into officer training a Lance Corporal and came out a Lieutenant, with the memory of forty-one funerals he hadn't attended.

**Term 3 (26–30):** Routine years, if any Marine year is routine. Garrison rotations, embassy security, a brushfire war on a world whose name he can't pronounce. He met Gunnery Sergeant Boro Halvik, his platoon sergeant — a barrel-chested veteran of three wars who taught him that an officer's job is to bring his people home. They became inseparable. Promoted Captain. For a brief season, Aldric believed the worst was behind him.

**Term 4 (30–34):** Counterinsurgency on Yelev, eighteen months of patrol-and-pacify in jungle that ate equipment. He lost men, but in ones and twos, the way an officer is supposed to — the bearable way. Met Brigadier Hesh Carrowen, the staff officer who would later sign his retirement papers and quietly recommend him for the file the Navy keeps of "useful retired officers." Came home with a chestful of ribbons and the sense that he had, perhaps, earned the right to a long life.

**Term 5 (34–38):** **The Kessar Drop.** Two companies — one-hundred-eighty-four Marines, including Halvik — inserted onto a research station that wasn't a research station. Whatever was in the lower levels killed them, or made them kill each other, or worse; the official report uses the phrase "catastrophic environmental compromise" forty-six times. Aldric was the only Marine to walk to the extraction point. He carried no one out because there was no one left to carry. The Imperium promoted him to Major two months later in a ceremony with no press, and the recording of the action is sealed at the Sector Command archive. His knees started clicking that year. He stopped sleeping through the night.

**Term 6 (38–42):** A staff posting at Sector HQ — the brass's way of keeping him alive and quiet. He planned operations he would never run, signed condolence letters in handwriting that had grown smaller and tighter, and learned the comms suites well enough to know when his own classified file was being accessed. He requested retirement at the end of the term. It was granted within the week, with a quiet pension and a quiet handshake from Brigadier Carrowen, who said only: *"Go find something to do that isn't this."*

### Backstory
Aldric Vehr walked away from twenty-four years in the Imperial Marines with every limb intact, a Major's pension, and the names of one-hundred-eighty-four dead Marines that he can recite in the order they fell at Kessar — a recitation he performs, alone, every night before sleep that does not come. The wound is not on his body; it is the quiet, unbearable mathematics of being the one who lived, and the growing suspicion that someone in the chain of command knew what was on that station before the drop order was signed. He has two Low Passages, two Ship Shares, and the unfocused, dangerous freedom of a man who has been told to *find something to do that isn't this* — and who is beginning to think that what he needs to do is find out who, exactly, sent his men down those stairs.
